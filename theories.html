Law of Universal Gravitation

Isaac Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation states that every mass attracts every other mass with a force proportional to their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. This law explained both earthly and celestial motions, unifying our understanding of gravity and laying the foundation for classical physics.

Euclid's Geomatry Theory

Euclid's geometry, presented in his work Elements, is built on a set of axioms and postulates, which are accepted as true without proof. From these, he logically derived theorems about points, lines, angles, and shapes. His method of proof, emphasizing logical deduction, became a cornerstone of mathematical reasoning. Euclid's work not only shaped the development of geometry but also set the standard for how mathematical principles are rigorously proven, influencing mathematics for over two thousand years.

Al-Khawari Theories

Al-Khwarizmi's key contribution is his development of algebra, outlined in Al-Kitab al-Mukhtasar fi Hisab al-Jabr wal-Muqabala. He introduced systematic methods for solving linear and quadratic equations, focusing on "completion" and "balancing." This approach shifted mathematics from geometry to algebra and laid the foundation for modern algebra, influencing both the Islamic world and Europe.
